## I. Names Given to the Same Event

Different schools insist on different titles. None agree on cause; all agree on consequence.

- **“The Emergence”** (Archives): neutral, descriptive.
- **“The First Opening”** (temple registries): implies a granted doorway.
- **“The Great Shiver”** (folk accounts): emphasizes felt bodily unease.
- **“The Unquiet Dawn”** (older marginalia): emphasizes the sense that “morning began” differently thereafter.

**[Consensus | Medium confidence]** These names cluster around the same span of days and the same pattern of reports: ordinary order persisted, but began to admit contradiction.

---

## II. What the Archives Are Willing to Claim

**[Record | High confidence]** The Emergence is not “the first time magic occurred.” It is the first time the world’s resistance to breach behaved as though a hidden threshold had been crossed.

The practical results, as later centuries can verify:
- small workings became repeatable,
- rites began to produce consistent outcomes *between practitioners*,
- and certain places acquired stable reputations as “easier” or “harder” for breach.

**[Inference | Medium confidence]** Something changed in the Weave’s tolerance—less like a collapse, more like a re-setting of what kinds of contradiction could be hosted without immediate reversion.

> [!archivist-note-left]
> The public prefers a story with a face. I prefer a story that predicts tomorrow.

## III. The Three Dominant Explanations (All Incomplete)

### Hypothesis A — Spontaneous Singularity in Weave Logic
**[Hypothesis | Low–Medium confidence]** The Weave experienced a local-to-global discontinuity: a moment where its internal accounting briefly failed, then restabilized into a new regime.

This hypothesis matches the **shape** of the chaos spike and the long aftermath of altered practice, but it is difficult to test without recreating what we should not recreate.

### Hypothesis B — Geologic / Place-Resonance Shift
**[Hypothesis | Medium confidence]** The “Emergence” may be partly a cartographic illusion: instruments and witnesses congregated around certain lands (notably the Summer Isles), and the world’s *place-accords* changed, not the world entire.

This would explain why the “breathing rhythm” persists across centuries of observation, and why it tracks certain regions more strongly than others.

### Hypothesis C — The Recognition Threshold
**[Inference | Medium confidence]** A breach becomes heavier when minds recognize it as real. A culture that crosses a recognition threshold (by teaching, recording, and enforcing a practice) may change the statistical ecology of breach without any single cosmic cause.

This explanation is socially irritating and therefore often ignored. It is also disturbingly consistent with what happens when a miracle becomes doctrine.

> [!archivist-note-right]
> If you want a villain: choose “curiosity.”
> If you want a mechanism: choose “repetition.”

## IV. Myth-Accounts Collected (Not Endorsed)

I include these because myths preserve *what people noticed* even when they misname the cause.

### The Temple Canticle (Common in City Shrines)
**[Myth | Low confidence]** The world “exhaled,” and the first prayers did not fall to the floor.

### The Fisherfolk Version (Coastal)
**[Myth | Low confidence]** The tide came in wrong—once—and every net after that remembered the wrongness.

### The Hearth Version (Domestic Ledgers, Unofficial)
**[Myth | Low confidence]** A home is safest when its rules are unspoken. During the Emergence, the rules were spoken aloud.

> [!archivist-note-left]
> I do not record myths to mock them. I record them because they are data written in the only ink most people possess.

## V. Why the Archives Care

**[Record | High confidence]** The Emergence is the opening chapter of every later disaster and every later safeguard.

If you want the smallest usable conclusion:

- The world can be breached.
- The breach leaves residue.
- The residue accumulates.

Everything else is commentary.
