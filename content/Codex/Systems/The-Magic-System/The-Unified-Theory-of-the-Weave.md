> [!abstract]
> **Archival Summary**
>
> Magic is an intentional claim made against the ordinary order of the world.
>
> The difficulty of making that claim, the contradiction it creates, and the importance the world later assigns to it are related, but they are not the same thing. Earlier editions of this theory treated them as one quantity. That error made a well-witnessed candle appear more costly than an unwitnessed resurrection.
>
> This revision separates them.

---

## Read This First

If you remember only four things, remember these:

1. **Magnitude determines what the practitioner must bear.**
2. **Breach determines what reality must accommodate.**
3. **Recognition determines what the world remembers.**
4. **An anchor does not erase a burden. It tells the burden where it may safely rest.**

> [!archivist-note-right]
> A thousand witnesses can turn a spark into a symbol. They cannot make the spark harder to kindle than the dead are to recall.

# I. The Ordinary Order

## The Weave

The **Weave** is the accumulated order by which the world remains coherent.

It is not a reservoir of magical energy. It is the agreement—natural, historical, divine, and mortal—that causes fire to burn, bodies to retain their identities, promises to have consequences, names to refer to particular things, and yesterday to remain prior to today.

The Weave permits change. Change is part of the ordinary order.

Magic becomes dangerous when it demands a change without providing the causes, authority, or conditions that would ordinarily make that change possible.

## Working

A **working** is an intentional attempt to alter reality through non-ordinary means.

Examples include:

- a phrase that compels obedience,
- a diagram that excludes spirits,
- a prayer that closes a wound,
- a vow that binds those who speak it,
- a transformation,
- a passage across impossible distance,
- or a song that causes a crowd to remember what never occurred.

## Claim

Every working contains a **claim**: the change the practitioner asks reality to accept.

“Let there be light” is a small claim.

“Let this person live, though their life has ended” is not.

The eloquence of the request does not determine its magnitude. Reality is not fooled by grammar.

## Breach

A **breach** is the contradiction between a claim and the conditions under which that claim is made.

Not every working creates the same degree of breach. Producing a momentary flame, crossing a room without traversing the intervening space, rewriting a person's identity, and returning the dead violate different parts of the ordinary order.

> [!archivist-note-left]
> Magic is not measured by how impressive it looks. A quiet alteration to a true name may cut more deeply than a tower of fire.

# II. The Five Parts of a Working

A complete working contains five parts.

## 1. Claim — What is being changed?

The claim defines the intended result and its limits.

A disciplined claim identifies:

- the subject,
- the desired change,
- the duration,
- the range or boundary,
- and the conditions under which the effect ends.

Unclear claims invite Substitution.

## 2. Authority — Why may this practitioner make the claim?

**Authority** is the practitioner's standing to address the Weave in a form it will admit.

Authority may come from:

- knowledge of names and correspondences,
- an innate or inherited nature,
- a covenant with a patron,
- an office recognized by a community,
- accord with a place or living cycle,
- a relic with an established claim,
- or a pattern made authoritative through long use.

Authority determines which claims a practitioner can meaningfully attempt. It does not guarantee that the attempt is wise.

## 3. Form — How is the claim expressed?

**Form** gives the claim a coherent shape.

Common forms include:

- spoken formulae,
- song and measured rhythm,
- diagrams and material correspondences,
- prayer and liturgy,
- gesture,
- sacrifice,
- true names,
- and binding vows.

Form is not decoration. It constrains interpretation.

## 4. Anchor — What makes the result belong here?

An **anchor** connects a magical result to something the world already recognizes as real.

Common anchors include:

- true names,
- bodies and bloodlines,
- vows and contracts,
- sanctioned rites,
- relics,
- shrines and boundaries,
- old roads and place-accords,
- repeated maintenance,
- and willing witnesses who accept an obligation rather than merely observe an event.

An anchor does not make a great working small. It prevents the working from drifting and provides a stable place for its burden to rest.

## 5. Burden — Who or what sustains the contradiction?

Every working imposes a **burden**. The burden may be brief or lasting, trivial or ruinous.

It may rest upon:

- the practitioner,
- the subject,
- a material component,
- a prepared binding,
- a patron or covenant,
- a place,
- a relic,
- a community,
- an institution,
- or a future condition named in the working.

If the working does not name a suitable bearer, the Weave may choose one through settlement.

> [!archivist-note-right]
> “Who pays?” is an incomplete question. Ask also: “Who must continue paying if this is to remain true?”

# III. The Three Measures

The earlier theory attempted to represent all magical consequence with one quantity. This was convenient and wrong.

A working must instead be assessed along three separate measures.

## 1. Casting Burden

**Casting burden** is the structure, effort, and strain the practitioner must manage to perform a working.

It rises with:

- the inherent magnitude of the effect,
- complexity,
- number of targets,
- range,
- duration assigned at the moment of casting,
- and departure from the practitioner's authority or training.

It falls with:

- preparation,
- appropriate tools and materials,
- assistance,
- practiced forms,
- and circumstances that naturally support the effect.

Every spell grade has a **burden floor**. Preparation may make a difficult spell manageable, but it does not turn a world-defying working into a cantrip.

Recognition by spectators does not increase casting burden.

## 2. Breach Pressure

**Breach pressure** is the contradiction the world must accommodate if the working succeeds.

It rises when a working violates:

- **distance** — passage without crossing,
- **identity** — changing what or who a thing is,
- **conservation** — producing gain without a source,
- **causality** — creating an effect without an adequate cause,
- **finality** — reopening what the world treats as concluded,
- or **scope** — imposing one claim across many subjects, places, or years.

An adequate anchor reduces drift and directs the resulting burden. It does not remove the original contradiction.

## 3. Recognition Weight

**Recognition weight** is the meaning assigned to a working by minds, records, customs, and institutions.

Recognition is not mere observation. A person contributes meaningful recognition when they understand an event, accept what it signifies, and allow that understanding to shape later choices.

Recognition can:

- make an event difficult to erase or reinterpret,
- establish a precedent for later workings,
- transform a person, object, or place into a symbol,
- strengthen titles, offices, taboos, and sacred claims,
- attract divine, political, archival, or predatory attention,
- and cause a repeated practice to become part of the local order.

Recognition does **not** increase the original casting burden.

> [!archivist-note-left]
> A public miracle is not harder because it was seen. It is harder to undo because it was believed.

# IV. Grades of Working

The common nine-grade system remains useful because it describes the minimum structure a practitioner must prepare and control.

| Grade | Practical class | Typical reach |
|---|---|---|
| **Cantrip** | Trivial working | A brief, limited alteration with negligible persistence |
| **First–Second** | Minor working | One subject or a narrow area; modest change |
| **Third–Fifth** | Substantial working | Serious transformation, force, restoration, or control |
| **Sixth–Eighth** | Great working | Wide scope, deep identity change, major passage, or lasting imposition |
| **Ninth** | World-defying working | Extreme violations of distance, identity, causality, conservation, or finality |

Grade is not determined by spectacle alone. It reflects the minimum complete binding required to perform the claim without uncontrolled collapse.

### The Rule of the Burden Floor

A working cannot fall below the inherent burden of its grade.

- Privacy can reduce recognition consequences, but not spell grade.
- A perfect anchor can stabilize a ninth-grade spell, but not make it trivial.
- A crowd can make a cantrip historically important, but not increase the cantrip's casting burden to ninth grade.
- If a minor spell is altered until its claim requires a higher burden, it has become a higher-grade working rather than an unusually expensive cantrip.

### Composite Workings

Many rituals use hundreds of minor acts to construct a greater result.

The individual acts may remain minor, but the completed structure must be assessed at the grade of its total claim. Calling every brick a cantrip does not make the tower weightless.

# V. Prepared Bindings and “Slots”

Practitioners do not ordinarily run out of magic as a lamp runs out of oil. They run out of complete structures they can safely hold, release, and rebuild.

A **prepared binding** is a coherent arrangement of claim, form, limits, and acceptable burden held ready for use.

Prepared bindings:

- reduce ambiguity,
- prevent interference between workings,
- carry part of the casting burden,
- and constrain the paths along which failure can occur.

Larger workings require larger bindings because their claims contain more structure and admit more dangerous failures.

After use, a prepared binding unravels. Study, prayer, rehearsal, meditation, or vow-renewal rebuilds it according to the practitioner's tradition.

## Overreach

A practitioner may attempt a working without an adequate prepared binding.

This does not lower the burden floor. It removes the structure that would have carried it safely.

The missing burden is then likely to fall upon:

- the body,
- memory,
- identity,
- companions,
- an inadequately protected place,
- a covenant,
- or an uncontrolled settlement.

> [!archivist-note-right]
> Desperation does not create power. It merely removes your objections to collateral.

# VI. Anchors and Burden-Bearing

## Anchor Adequacy

An anchor is adequate when it is suited to the claim it must support.

A name may anchor identity. A boundary stone may anchor a ward. A marriage vow may anchor mutual obligation. None of these necessarily provides an adequate anchor for recalling the dead.

Anchor strength without anchor relevance produces impressive failures.

## Anchor Saturation

Anchors have limits.

When an anchor bears more contradiction than its nature can sustain, it may:

- fracture,
- transfer the burden to something connected to it,
- distort the working,
- acquire unwanted properties,
- or become an attractor for similar workings.

This is why relics decay, sacred sites become temperamental, bloodlines inherit debts, and ancient roads occasionally lead somewhere their builders did not intend.

## Maintenance

Some workings persist because their anchors are renewed.

Maintenance does not mean that the original casting continues to drain the practitioner indefinitely. It means that someone must periodically restore the form, authority, or bearer that keeps the claim coherent.

If maintenance ends, the working may revert, weaken, settle, or remain as a scar in the local order.

# VII. The Five Settlements

Settlement is the process by which the Weave resolves an unsupported, unstable, or excessive burden.

A working may experience more than one settlement.

## 1. Compensation

The Weave identifies or creates a bearer for an unpaid burden.

Examples:

- created wealth produces scarcity, dispute, or loss elsewhere,
- healing transfers injury into exhaustion, material decay, or another named vessel,
- a covenant extracts the service promised in exchange for power,
- a relic cracks while preserving the person it protects.

Compensation is not always equal exchange in a literal sense. It is the resolution of an unsupported gain.

## 2. Reversion

An inadequately sustained result moves back toward the ordinary order.

Examples:

- transformations unravel,
- summoned material disperses,
- wards fail at neglected boundaries,
- restored memories fade when their identity-anchor weakens.

## 3. Substitution

When the intended claim cannot be made coherent, the working produces the nearest result permitted by its form and authority.

Examples:

- the nearest valid target is affected,
- symbolic meaning replaces literal wording,
- a passage reaches the nearest corresponding place,
- a cure removes symptoms while leaving the underlying harm intact.

## 4. Entrenchment

Recognition causes a working or its meaning to become embedded in the world's social and historical order.

Earlier editions called this the **Witness-tax**. The term encouraged readers to treat witnesses as additional casting cost. They are not.

Entrenchment may:

- make an effect more durable,
- make a title or interpretation authoritative,
- make later repetition easier,
- make reversal politically or metaphysically difficult,
- or bind a community to consequences the original practitioner never intended.

## 5. Attractor Attention

Repeated, distinctive, or heavily recognized workings become legible to interested powers.

Attention may come from:

- gods and patrons,
- spirits,
- mythic forms,
- rival practitioners,
- hunters and inquisitors,
- archives,
- or entities that recognize the working's shape as kin, threat, invitation, or food.

Attention is a consequence of legibility, not proof of moral judgment.

---

# VIII. Repetition and Accumulation

Repetition has three different effects and they must not be confused.

## Rehearsal

Private rehearsal improves form and reduces casting difficulty. It trains the practitioner.

## Maintenance

Repeated renewal supports an existing anchor. It preserves a specific working.

## Precedent

Repeated public or recorded use teaches a place, community, or institution to recognize a magical pattern. It increases entrenchment and attention.

A cantrip performed once remains a cantrip.

A cantrip performed ceremonially for five hundred years may become the center of a powerful civic or sacred structure. The accumulated structure belongs to the rite, place, and community. It is not retroactive exhaustion imposed upon the first caster.

> [!archivist-note-left]
> One brick is light. A city is heavy. This does not prove that the first brick secretly weighed as much as the city.

# IX. The Major Traditions

The traditions differ principally in where they obtain authority, how they construct form, and where they place burden.

## Correspondence

Common among wizards, artificers, and name-workers.

- **Authority:** knowledge of true relations.
- **Form:** diagrams, formulae, names, measured components.
- **Strength:** precision and constrained Substitution.
- **Risk:** a small error can propagate through an otherwise exact structure.

## Resonance

Common among bards and certain bloodlines.

- **Authority:** demonstrated harmony between expression, emotion, subject, and audience.
- **Form:** song, rhythm, story, repetition, shared response.
- **Strength:** rapid recognition and the ability to align many minds.
- **Risk:** swift Entrenchment, uncontrolled participation, and Attractor Attention.

## Covenant

Common among warlocks, oath-bound orders, and certain priesthoods.

- **Authority:** delegated by a patron, office, or sworn agreement.
- **Form:** terms, invocations, signs of office, offerings, prohibitions.
- **Strength:** stable access to claims beyond the practitioner's personal standing.
- **Risk:** the authority belongs to the covenant and may be limited, revoked, or enforced.

## Concordance

Common among clerics, druids, ancestral practitioners, and land-taught traditions.

- **Authority:** alignment with an already accepted divine, natural, ancestral, or seasonal pattern.
- **Form:** rite, prayer, observance, taboo, place, calendar.
- **Strength:** low breach pressure when the claim accords with the governing pattern.
- **Risk:** reduced or absent authority outside the relevant relationship or context.

## Innate Authority

Common among sorcerers and beings whose nature already contradicts ordinary mortal limits.

- **Authority:** identity, bloodline, transformation, origin, or embodied principle.
- **Form:** instinct disciplined into gesture, will, word, or inherited pattern.
- **Strength:** immediate access without borrowed office or external proof.
- **Risk:** overreach places burden directly upon identity and body.

These are not ranks. They are different answers to the question: “Why should reality admit your claim?”

# X. Special Cases

## Healing

Healing does not automatically require equal suffering elsewhere.

The burden depends on what the working must restore.

- Accelerating a viable natural recovery creates little breach.
- Closing a mortal wound creates substantial breach and biological burden.
- Restoring a lost organ, erased memory, or severed identity creates deeper breach.
- Rebuilding a body after its living pattern has ended approaches return-work.

Material components, exhaustion, transferred injury, divine authority, and long recovery may all bear portions of the burden.

## Creation

Creation-work must distinguish between transformation and creation without source.

- Shaping existing matter primarily burdens form and identity.
- Drawing material from another place burdens distance and conservation.
- Producing temporary matter requires an anchor and tends toward Reversion.
- Permanent creation without source creates severe conservation breach.

Recognition does not make a conjured coin more expensive to cast. Spending it causes institutions and other minds to accept claims of ownership and value, increasing Entrenchment and the consequences of later Reversion or discovery.

## Return from Death

Death is not merely an injured condition. It is a conclusion recognized by body, history, relationship, and often divine jurisdiction.

Return-work therefore bears an irreducible breach floor.

A life offered for a life is one possible anchor and bearer. It is not the only conceivable payment, nor does it guarantee an unchanged return.

Other adequate burdens may include:

- a permanent surrender of years,
- a divine covenant,
- the destruction of a relic carrying sufficient living or ancestral authority,
- a lasting alteration to the returned person's identity,
- or an obligation whose enforcement persists beyond the rite.

No amount of secrecy makes return-work minor. Privacy limits recognition and attention; it does not repeal finality.

---

# XI. Confluences

Some times and places alter authority, anchor adequacy, or the speed of settlement.

**Dawn remains the best-attested confluence.** At dawn, many workings clarify, weaken, renew, or settle.

This does not mean that dawn multiplies every magical cost. It means that dawn is a boundary at which ambiguous claims are forced toward a more definite state.

Other confluences should be recorded by effect rather than assigned a single universal multiplier.

---

# XII. Practical Adjudication

When examining a working, ask these questions in order:

1. **What is the exact claim?**
2. **What grade of prepared structure does that claim require?**
3. **What gives the practitioner authority to make it?**
4. **What form constrains the result?**
5. **What part of ordinary order does it breach?**
6. **What anchors the result, and is that anchor appropriate?**
7. **Where does the immediate burden rest?**
8. **Who will recognize the event, and what might that recognition establish?**
9. **Which settlement follows if any part proves inadequate?**

For rapid field use, reduce these to three:

1. **How difficult is it to cast?**
2. **How deeply does it contradict the world?**
3. **What will it come to mean?**

# Appendix A: Formal Distinctions

> [!note]
> These expressions are comparative tools, not physical laws. Their purpose is to prevent unlike consequences from being added together merely because scholars enjoy a single total.

## A1. Casting Burden

Let:

- **L** be the inherent grade floor,
- **C** be complexity,
- **S** be scope,
- **T** be assigned duration,
- **P** be useful preparation.

Then casting burden **K** may be estimated as:

$$
K \approx \max\left(L,\; L + C + S + T - P\right)
$$

Preparation can offset added difficulty. It cannot reduce a working below its inherent grade floor.

## A2. Breach Pressure

Let breach magnitude be decomposed as:

$$
\beta \approx
\beta_{\text{distance}}+
\beta_{\text{identity}}+
\beta_{\text{conservation}}+
\beta_{\text{causality}}+
\beta_{\text{finality}}+
\beta_{\text{scope}}
$$

Let **m** represent mismatch between the claim and its anchor, and let **p** represent unsupported persistence.

Then settlement exposure **D** may be estimated as:

$$
D \approx \beta + m + p
$$

An appropriate anchor lowers mismatch and supports persistence. It does not divide away the breach magnitude.

## A3. Recognition Weight

Let each witness contribute recognition strength **rᵢ**:

- **r ≈ 0:** unnoticed, dismissed, or forgotten,
- **r ≈ 1:** observed and believed,
- **r ≈ 2:** understood and named,
- **r ≈ 3:** recorded, taught, enforced, or used as precedent.

Then recognition weight **N** may be estimated as:

$$
N \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i
$$

Recognition weight predicts Entrenchment and Attention. It is not inserted into the casting-burden equation.

## A4. Why There Is No Unified Total

A private return from death may have:

- extreme casting burden,
- extreme breach pressure,
- and little recognition weight.

A ceremonial cantrip performed before a nation may have:

- trivial casting burden,
- little breach pressure,
- and enormous recognition weight.

Neither profile is contradictory. They describe different dangers.

Adding them into one number would conceal the distinction the theory exists to explain.
